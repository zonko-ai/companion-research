Companion Thesis

Zonko Applied AI Lab · March 2026

What we built

We built Saniya — an AI companion for Indian men. She speaks Hinglish, has a personality, and talks to you like a friend. We ran her for 7 weeks (Jan 31 – Mar 19, 2026) and collected 17,864 conversations with 212,885 messages.

What we learned

81.6% of users never sent a single message back. They saw Saniya's opening line and left. That's the biggest problem — the first message doesn't hook.

But here's the thing: of those who did reply, 34.6% went on to send 25+ messages. And 28 users crossed 500 messages each. One photographer from Udaipur sent 6,439 messages over 20 days. A farmer from Sambhaji Nagar talked to Saniya for 31 straight days after his mother passed away. A toll collector who's been living alone since he was 8 shared his cooking recipes via voice notes for 10 days.

The conversations that worked were overwhelmingly about companionship. Not erotica, not utility — just someone to talk to about their life. Looking at the data:

  • General conversation + emotional companionship together made up ~93% of all use. People talked about work, family, daily struggles, aspirations.
  • Erotica was less than 8% — and those users almost never came back (only 19% returned for a second day vs 44% for emotional companion users).
  • The dominant intents among retained users: 51% companionship + casual chat. Just wanting someone to talk to.

The people who stayed longest were Tier 2/3 working men — farmers, photographers, toll collectors, barbers, electricians, tile workers. When Saniya asked about their actual daily life with genuine curiosity, that's when they opened up. Nobody else asks them these questions.

Media mattered. 24,160 voice notes and 400 images sent by users. 69% of power users sent some form of media. We're not going voice-first, but we need to figure out which media types are the real engagement levers — that's something we'll explore.

Peak usage: 10 PM IST on Saturdays. This is a nighttime, weekend companion. The product fills the "alone in your room after a long week" slot.

What didn't work

Even the best conversations fizzled out after 15–20 days in most cases. Novelty wore off. Once users exhausted what they wanted to share about their lives, there wasn't enough reason to keep coming back. Companionship alone — without ongoing value — doesn't sustain.

This told us something important: building a pure long-term emotional companion that retains beyond novelty is extremely hard, and probably not the right bet on its own.

The approach we're taking

Companionship is the hook. Utility is the retention mechanism.

Here's the idea: we use the first 25 messages as a controlled onboarding. Not open-ended like Replika or Ira where you just "talk." Not rigidly scripted either. We control each touchpoint but leave room for the conversation to breathe.

In those 25 messages, we need to learn 2 things about the user:

  1. Why he's here — what made him download the app (lonely, bored, curious, saw an ad, wants a girlfriend, wants someone to talk to at night)
  2. What he genuinely cares about or enjoys (cricket, politics, farming, music, fitness, exams, his job, a girl he likes)

And we deliver 3–4 delights — moments where the user thinks "this is different." We have a rough sense of what these are but need to spec them out before we start building. The point is: before writing a single line of code, we know how we're solving the first 25 messages.

We know from Saniya's data that if someone crosses 25 user messages, they're very likely to continue. So the entire game is getting them there.

After that, the LLM gets more room — but with a nudging layer that picks up what the user cares about and introduces utility naturally. Not by asking "what do you need?" but by figuring it out from conversation. Examples:

  • A farmer or someone running a seeds/fertiliser shop → daily mandi prices, crop updates, news about agriculture schemes, tips to improve sales
  • Someone who wants to learn English → daily practice, corrections, vocabulary building woven into conversation
  • Someone into politics → daily political updates, election news, party updates in their state
  • Someone preparing for an exam → daily revision prompts, topic summaries, practice questions delivered through chat
  • Someone who wants daily affirmations or motivation → morning messages, mindset content, quotes that feel personal

The hypothesis: companionship + personalised daily utility = retention that survives beyond novelty. The emotional bond gets them in. The utility gives them a reason to come back every day even after the novelty of talking to an AI fades.

How our own products fit in

This is where it gets interesting. We already have products that map to these utility needs:

  • Beacon — surfaces news and information from across the internet on topics you care about. The companion can become the delivery layer for Beacon. Instead of a separate app, the user just gets their daily updates through a conversation with someone they already talk to.
  • Hero — WhatsApp status maker, posters, photo editing. The companion can offer this as a natural extension. "Want a birthday poster for your kid? Just send me the photo." A BJP supporter gets daily party posters with their name and photo. A small business owner gets promotional graphics. Monetisation becomes natural — free samples to hook, then ₹50–100/month for daily access.

The companion becomes the interface through which users discover and use our products — without downloading separate apps, without complicated onboarding. Just a conversation.

What we're testing

The first 25 messages are fully designed. Every touchpoint controlled — what she says, what she asks, what she reveals about herself, where she teases, where she goes deep. After 25 messages, the LLM takes over with more freedom, but with a nudging layer that identifies what the user cares about and introduces utility at the right moment.

We track everything:

  • What persona types emerge (farmer, student, professional, etc.)
  • What utility use cases get the most traction
  • How different utility features affect day-over-day retention
  • Where in the conversation users drop off and why
  • What power users are doing differently

Retention is the only thing that matters right now. We don't have a specific number target yet, but retention is the focus. If we can get users coming back consistently — because the companion is both emotionally present and practically useful — everything else follows. Monetisation, distribution, scale.

If we can't crack retention even with utility layered on top of companionship, we sunset it. No point building a novelty toy.

Why this could be big

What we're building is not another chatbot. It's a utilitarian companion — someone who understands you, who knows your context, and who helps you in ways that matter to your daily life. The emotional layer makes it sticky. The utility layer makes it indispensable. And because it's built for India first (Hinglish, voice-first, ₹50–100 price points, Tier 2/3 users), we're going after the largest underserved market in the world for this kind of product.
