Companion Thesis

Zonko Applied AI Lab · March 2026

We built Saniya. AI companion, Hinglish, female persona. Ran her for 7 weeks. 17,864 conversations, 212k messages.

81.6% of users never replied to the first message. Just didn't care. But here's the thing — of the ones who did reply, 34.6% sent 25+ messages. 28 users crossed 500. One guy from Udaipur (photographer) sent 6,439 messages in 20 days. A farmer talked to her for 31 days straight after his mother died. A toll collector who's lived alone since he was 8 sent cooking recipes via voice notes for 10 days.

~93% of conversations were about companionship. Work, family, daily life, aspirations. Erotica was less than 8% — and those users barely came back (19% return vs 44% for emotional companion users). The people who actually stayed were Tier 2/3 working men. Farmers, barbers, electricians, photographers. When Saniya asked about their actual life with real curiosity, they opened up. Nobody else asks them these questions.

Media mattered. 24,160 voice notes, 400 images. 69% of power users sent some kind of media. We're not going voice-first, but we need to figure out which media types are the real engagement levers — that's something we'll explore.

Peak usage: 10 PM on Saturdays. This is a nighttime thing. Alone after a long week.

What didn't work: even the best conversations fizzled after 15–20 days. Novelty wore off. Once someone had shared their life story, there wasn't a reason to come back the next day. Companionship alone doesn't retain beyond novelty. That's the core learning.

So here's what we're doing now.

Companionship is the hook. Utility is the retention.

The first 25 messages are the whole game. We know from the data that if someone crosses 25 user messages, they're very likely to keep going. So we're designing those 25 messages deliberately. Not fully open-ended like Replika or Ira where you just... talk. But not rigidly scripted either. We control each touchpoint while leaving room for the conversation to feel natural.

In those 25 messages, we need to learn 2 things:

  1. Why he's here — what made him download the app (lonely, bored, curious, saw an ad, wants a girlfriend, wants someone to talk to at night)
  2. What he genuinely cares about or enjoys (cricket, politics, farming, music, fitness, exams, his job, a girl he likes)

And we deliver 3–4 delights. Moments where the user goes "oh wait, this is different." We have a rough sense of what those are but need to spec them out properly. The point is — before we write a single line of code, we know how we're solving the first 25 messages.

After that, the LLM gets more room. But there's a nudging layer underneath that picks up what the user cares about and starts introducing utility — not by asking "what do you need" but by figuring it out from the conversation and sliding it in naturally. Like:

  • Someone who farms or runs a seed shop — daily mandi prices, crop updates, govt schemes
  • Someone who wants to learn English — practice woven into regular conversation
  • Someone into politics — daily updates about their state, their party
  • Someone prepping for an exam — revision prompts, topic summaries through chat
  • Someone who wants a birthday poster for their kid — "just send me the photo" and she makes it

Core hypothesis: companionship + daily utility = retention that survives novelty. The emotional bond gets them in the door. The utility gives them a reason to come back when the charm of talking to an AI wears off.

The nice thing is — we already have products that plug in here.

Beacon surfaces news and info on topics you care about. Instead of a separate app, the companion just becomes Beacon's delivery layer. Your daily updates come from someone you already talk to.

Hero makes WhatsApp posters, status images, photo edits. A BJP supporter gets daily party posters with his name and photo. A small business gets promotional graphics. Free ones to hook, ₹50–100/month after that. No separate app, no onboarding. Just "want this? send me the photo."

The companion becomes the front door for everything Zonko builds.

What we're actually tracking: persona types that emerge, which utility features get traction, how retention changes feature by feature, where people drop off, what power users do differently. It's both a product and a consumer insights engine.

Retention is the only thing that matters right now. We don't have a specific number yet but that's the north star. If we can get people coming back consistently — because the companion is both emotionally there and practically useful — everything else follows. Monetisation, distribution, scale.

If we can't crack retention even with utility on top of companionship — we sunset it. No point building a novelty toy.

What we're actually building: a utilitarian companion. Someone who gets you, knows your context, and does something useful for you every day. Not another chatbot. The emotional layer makes it sticky. The utility layer makes it indispensable.
